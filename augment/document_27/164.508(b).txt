# Law
164.508(b)
# Key Legal Concepts
Valid authorizations: Authorizations must meet specific criteria to be considered valid, ensuring that individuals' rights regarding their health information are protected. Defective authorizations: An authorization is deemed invalid if it contains defects, highlighting the importance of proper documentation and accuracy in health information management. Compound authorizations: Authorizations cannot be combined with other documents, which prevents confusion and ensures that individuals clearly understand what they are consenting to. Prohibition on conditioning: Individuals cannot be coerced into providing authorization as a condition for receiving medical treatment or benefits, safeguarding their autonomy. Revocation of authorizations: Individuals maintain the right to revoke their consent at any time, emphasizing ongoing control over their personal health information. Documentation requirements: Covered entities must keep records of authorizations, ensuring compliance and accountability in handling sensitive health information.
# Key Legal Principles
Patient consent: The requirement for valid authorizations embodies the principle of informed consent, ensuring that individuals are aware of and agree to how their health information is used. Privacy and confidentiality: The law underscores the importance of protecting individuals' privacy by regulating how their health information can be disclosed. Autonomy: By prohibiting conditioning and allowing revocation, the law reinforces the principle of individual autonomy in health care decisions.
# Application Scenarios
A healthcare provider must obtain a valid authorization from a patient before sharing their medical records with a third party, such as an insurance company. A patient decides to revoke their authorization for a specific disclosure, and the healthcare provider must ensure the revocation is documented to comply with the law.
